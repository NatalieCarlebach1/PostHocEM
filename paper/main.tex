\begin{abstract}
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) methods for medical image segmentation discard the unlabeled data once training converges, even though the model's predictions on it still contain residual uncertainty concentrated at organ boundaries.
We show that this residual entropy is spatially structured and can be exploited post-hoc.
\textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)} takes any converged SSL checkpoint and fine-tunes it for $2$--$5$ epochs using only entropy minimization on unlabeled data, with no labels, no pseudo-labels, and no architectural changes.
Applied to the publicly released BCP~\cite{bai2023bcp} checkpoints without test-set hyperparameter tuning, PEM improves Dice by $+1.12$ on Pancreas-CT (20\% labels), $+1.43$ on LA (5\%), and $+0.64$ on LA (10\%), and reduces the 95\% Hausdorff distance by $27\%$, $43\%$, and $30\%$ respectively, in $2.6$--$5.1$\,min per configuration on a single GPU.
Against three controlled post-hoc baselines --- temperature scaling, pseudo-label fine-tuning, and self-consistency --- PEM attains the largest mean gain on the two less-saturated configurations and is statistically indistinguishable from pseudo-label fine-tuning on the most saturated one (LA 10\%).
Temperature scaling produces no Dice change ($|\Delta|\!\le\!0.03$), indicating that PEM's gains are attributable to parameter updates at decision-boundary voxels rather than to recalibration.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Semi-supervised learning, medical image segmentation, entropy minimization, post-hoc fine-tuning
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Recent SSL methods for medical image segmentation~\cite{tarvainen2017meanteacher,yu2019uamt,bai2023bcp,liu2024most,assefa2025dycon} share a common operating assumption: once training converges, the unlabeled data is discarded and the final checkpoint is used as-is.
We make a complementary empirical observation.
Measuring the per-voxel softmax distribution of the publicly released BCP checkpoints on the unlabeled training set, we find that \textbf{$89$--$95\%$ of their total prediction entropy is concentrated in a thin uncertain-voxel shell ($5$--$9\%$ of the volume), whereas a supervised-only baseline trained on the same labels carries only $\sim$55\% of its entropy in the corresponding shell} (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}).
The $34$--$40$\,percentage-point gap observed on the checkpoints we study is a property of SSL convergence rather than of the network or the data, and it is the precondition that makes a post-hoc, label-free refinement step well-conditioned.
We exploit this structure with \textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)}: take any converged SSL checkpoint and fine-tune it for $2$--$5$ epochs on unlabeled data with a single entropy loss, optionally restricted to a confidence shell --- no labels, no pseudo-labels, and no architectural changes.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \setlength\itemsep{0pt}
    \item \textbf{An empirical regularity of SSL convergence.} Direct measurement (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}) shows that the three BCP checkpoints we study concentrate $89$--$95\%$ of their residual entropy in a small uncertain-voxel shell, while a supervised-only baseline trained on the same $12$ labels concentrates only $55\%$. To our knowledge, this is the first quantitative characterization of the residual uncertainty distribution in converged SSL segmentation checkpoints on these benchmarks.
    \item \textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization in a new operating regime.} We introduce PEM, a training-free, label-free post-processing step. PEM differs from training-time entropy minimization~\cite{grandvalet2004entropy,sohn2020fixmatch} in \textit{operating regime} rather than in loss: it acts on a converged optimum whose residual entropy is already structurally concentrated, so the gradient signal is automatically aligned with the locus of error and does not interact with the base model's pseudo-label loop or supervised gradients.
    \item \textbf{Reproducible empirical validation.} Under a fixed-budget protocol with no test-set hyperparameter tuning (mean over 3 seeds), PEM improves Dice by $+0.6$ to $+1.4$ and HD95 by $27$--$43\%$ on Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, and LA 10\% (Tab.~\ref{tab:main}); all three Dice gains exceed $3\sigma$ of seed variability and pass a per-case Wilcoxon signed-rank test at $p<0.025$. The learning-rate, masking-threshold, and epoch-budget ablations (Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}), together with the failure of PEM on a supervised-only baseline, verify the operating-regime argument.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Relation to classical entropy minimization.}
Entropy minimization itself is classical; its application as a post-hoc refinement of converged 3D segmentation checkpoints is not.
Standard entropy minimization acts during training on diffuse, evolving distributions, whereas a converged SSL checkpoint is in a qualitatively different regime: its per-voxel entropy is already concentrated in a thin boundary shell (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}), so the same loss admits a structurally aligned gradient that requires no explicit masking or class-balance regularizer.
Applied outside this regime --- to a supervised-only baseline whose entropy is diffuse --- the loss fails to improve the model.
Our contribution is the operating regime and its empirical characterization, not the loss function.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\vspace{-2pt}

\paragraph{Definition: shell entropy concentration $\rho_f(\tau)$.}
For a converged checkpoint $f_{\theta^*}$ on an unlabeled volume $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}$, write the per-voxel softmax $p_i = \mathrm{softmax}(f_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{x})_i)$, the per-voxel entropy $H_i = -\sum_c p_{i,c}\log p_{i,c}$, and the per-voxel confidence $c_i = \max_c p_{i,c}$.
We define the \textbf{shell entropy concentration} at confidence level $\tau \in (0,1]$ as
\begin{equation}
    \rho_f(\tau) \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{U}}\!\big[\sum_{i} H_i(\mathbf{x})\,\mathbb{1}[c_i(\mathbf{x})\le\tau]\big]}{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{U}}\!\big[\sum_{i} H_i(\mathbf{x})\big]} \;\in\; [0,1].
    \label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
$\rho_f(\tau)$ is the fraction of total prediction entropy carried by voxels whose maximum class probability is at most $\tau$.
We say $f$ has \textbf{boundary-structured residual entropy} at level $\tau$ if $\rho_f(\tau)\ge\rho^\star$; throughout this paper we use $\tau{=}0.99$ and $\rho^\star{=}0.85$ (justified empirically below).

\paragraph{Properties of $\rho_f(\tau)$.}
$\rho_f(\tau)$ is (i) \textit{label-free}, requiring only a forward pass over unlabeled data; (ii) \textit{self-localizing}, since on a well-converged segmentation network the only voxels with $c_i\!\le\!\tau$ lie on or near a decision boundary; (iii) \textit{operationally aligned with PEM}, because the per-voxel PEM gradient $\partial H_i/\partial p_i$ vanishes as $c_i\!\to\!1$, so a high $\rho_f(\tau)$ implies that the gradient signal of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PEM}}$ is automatically dominated by the boundary shell; and (iv) \textit{scalar}, comparable across networks, datasets, and label fractions.

\paragraph{Measured shell concentration.}
Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy} reports $\rho_f(0.99)$ for all checkpoints we evaluate.
The three BCP checkpoints concentrate $\rho_f(0.99)\in\{0.894,\,0.948,\,0.945\}$ on Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, and LA 10\% respectively, while a supervised-only VNet trained on the same $12$ labeled Pancreas-CT volumes attains only $\rho_f(0.99)\!=\!0.552$, with uncertain voxels lying on average $14.5$ voxels from any decision boundary.
The resulting $34$--$40$\,percentage-point gap is the precondition under which PEM is well-conditioned.

\paragraph{PEM-suitability diagnostic.}
We use $\rho_f(0.99)$ as a label-free suitability check: a single forward pass over the unlabeled training set suffices.
On the four checkpoints of Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}, the threshold $\rho^\star\!=\!0.85$ separates the three SSL checkpoints (which gain $+0.6$ to $+1.4$ Dice under PEM) from the supervised baseline ($\rho_f\!=\!0.552$, no gain).
We recommend reporting $\rho_f(0.99)$ alongside any released SSL checkpoint to indicate whether PEM is expected to apply.

\paragraph{Loss.}
Given an unlabeled volume $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}$ with voxel softmax outputs $p_i$ and per-voxel entropy $H_i = -\sum_c p_{i,c} \log p_{i,c}$, PEM minimizes
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{PEM}} =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}}
    \frac{\sum_{i} m_i\, H_i(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_i m_i + \epsilon},
    \quad
    m_i = \mathbb{1}[\max_c p_{i,c} \le \tau], \;\; \tau\in(0,1].
    \label{eq:pem}
\end{equation}
$\tau\!=\!1$ recovers full-volume entropy minimization; $\tau\!<\!1$ restricts the loss to the boundary shell.
PEM differs from training-time entropy minimization~\citep{grandvalet2004entropy,sohn2020fixmatch} in two respects.
\textit{(i)} It operates on a converged checkpoint, where the residual entropy is already concentrated at decision boundaries (verified above), so the gradient signal is structurally aligned with the locus of error.
\textit{(ii)} It introduces no pseudo-labels, no consistency regularizer, and no curriculum, so it does not interact with the base model's training dynamics or pseudo-label propagation.

\paragraph{Theoretical grounding: cluster assumption at the organ surface.}
The semi-supervised cluster assumption~\cite{chapelle2006ssl} states that classes form dense clusters in feature space and that the optimal decision boundary lies in the low-density regions between them.
Minimizing the conditional entropy $H(Y\!\mid\!X)$ on unlabeled data implements this principle by maximizing the classifier margin around its current decision surface~\cite{grandvalet2004entropy}.
For a binary voxel classifier with logit $z$ and probability $p\!=\!\sigma(z)$, the entropy gradient is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial H(p)}{\partial z} \;=\; -\,z\,p(1-p),
    \label{eq:dHdz}
\end{equation}
so a gradient step in the direction of decreasing $H$ moves $z$ further from zero with step size proportional to $p(1\!-\!p)$.
Two consequences are specific to 3D segmentation.
\textit{First}, the update vanishes at saturated voxels ($p\!\to\!0$ or $p\!\to\!1$) and is largest at $p\!=\!0.5$, so the $p(1\!-\!p)$ factor in Eq.~\ref{eq:dHdz} functions as an implicit boundary-shell mask that is functionally equivalent to the explicit threshold in Eq.~\ref{eq:pem}; no class-balance regularizer is needed to prevent collapse to a single class.
\textit{Second}, in 3D segmentation the feature-space decision surface is spatially co-located with the \textit{organ surface}, because boundary voxels are precisely where the per-voxel feature distribution is bimodal (partial-volume averaging, scanner blur, anatomical variability).
Under this alignment, PEM refines each boundary voxel toward the locally higher-probability class, sliding the predicted organ surface along the low-density direction in feature space.
When the alignment fails --- as in the supervised-only baseline, whose feature-space boundary is not co-located with the organ surface --- sharpening propagates the base model's topological errors instead of refining a near-correct contour (failure mode F1 in Sec.~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
\textbf{Checkpoints and splits.}
We fine-tune the publicly released BCP~\cite{bai2023bcp} checkpoints for Pancreas-CT~\cite{roth2015pancreas} (VNet, instance norm, 12 labeled / 50 unlabeled / 18 test) and LA 2018 (VNet, batch norm, 4 or 8 labeled / rest unlabeled / 20 test), using BCP's own preprocessing and canonical splits so the baseline numbers are an exact reproduction.
\textbf{Inputs.}
Training patches are $96^3$ (Pancreas) and $112\!\times\!112\!\times\!80$ (LA), center-cropped from the preprocessed H5 volumes, with batch size $B\!=\!2$ and a seed-dependent shuffled loader (no flip, no random crop).
This yields $\{25,38,36\}$ gradient steps per epoch on \{Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, LA 10\%\} respectively.
\textbf{Optimizer.}
Adam with no weight decay and no learning-rate schedule, $\ell_2$ gradient clipping at norm $1.0$, and a dataset-specific LR chosen once via 5-fold cross-validation on the labeled training set ($5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for Pancreas-CT, $1\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for LA; gentler on LA because its base model is more saturated).
For LA's BatchNorm backbone we freeze all BN running statistics; the BN affine parameters remain trainable.
\textbf{Schedule.}
The number of PEM epochs is fixed in advance at $E\!=\!2$ (Pancreas-CT) and $E\!=\!5$ (LA), giving a total of $50$, $190$, and $180$ parameter updates per run on the three configurations; no quantity is read from the test set during fine-tuning.
The PEM rows of Tab.~\ref{tab:main} are mean$\pm$std over three random seeds (2020, 42, 123); the baselines in Tab.~\ref{tab:main} and the ablations in Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation} use seed $2020$.
\textbf{Evaluation.}
Sliding-window inference with BCP's original parameters: $96^3$ patches and stride $(16,16,4)$ on Pancreas-CT, $112\!\times\!112\!\times\!80$ patches and stride $(18,18,4)$ on LA.
Softmax probabilities are averaged over overlapping windows and the final label map is the voxel-wise argmax; no test-time augmentation.
\textbf{Hardware and runtime.}
All experiments run on a single \textbf{NVIDIA RTX 4070 Ti SUPER} (16\,GB) with Python 3.12 and PyTorch 2.9.1\,$+$\,cu128, with \texttt{cudnn.deterministic=True} and \texttt{cudnn.benchmark=False}.
Per-configuration wall-clock (checkpoint load, baseline evaluation, and $E$ PEM epochs each with a per-epoch evaluation) measured on the single-seed runs is $5.1$\,min for Pancreas-CT 20\%, $2.7$\,min for LA 5\%, and $2.6$\,min for LA 10\%.
The full experimental grid --- three-seed PEM, three post-hoc baselines (TS, PL-FT, SC), and the full LR\,$\times\tau\,\times E$ ablation on LA 5\% (Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}) --- completes in under four GPU-hours total.
Code, trained checkpoints, split files, and per-run logs will be released at \url{<anonymized>}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
\vspace{-2pt}

\paragraph{Comparison scope.}
We compare against BCP because it is the only recent SSL method on these benchmarks that releases pretrained checkpoints; MOST~\cite{liu2024most} and DyCON~\cite{assefa2025dycon} provide only training code and we could not faithfully reproduce their reported scores within our time budget.
Evaluating PEM on top of more recent SSL checkpoints, once available, is a natural follow-up.
See Section~\ref{sec:method}, ``Implementation details'', for datasets, splits, optimizer, evaluation protocol, hardware, and per-configuration runtime.

\paragraph{Post-hoc baselines.}
To isolate \textit{which} aspect of PEM is responsible for the gains, we add three post-hoc baselines that operate on the same BCP checkpoint with the same fixed-budget protocol:
(\textbf{TS}) \textit{Temperature scaling} freezes all weights and optimizes a single scalar $T$ to minimize the entropy of $\mathrm{softmax}(\text{logits}/T)$ on the unlabeled set; this is a degenerate ablation of PEM with only one trainable parameter and is theoretically unable to change argmax;
(\textbf{PL-FT}) \textit{pseudo-label fine-tune} freezes a teacher copy, generates hard pseudo-labels via argmax with largest-connected-component filtering (matching BCP's own pseudo-label procedure), and fine-tunes the student with supervised CE$+$Dice;
(\textbf{SC}) \textit{self-consistency} (post-hoc Mean Teacher) keeps a frozen teacher and minimizes the MSE between the student forward on a noised input and the teacher forward on the clean input.
All three share PEM's epoch budget, learning rate, optimizer, data, and protocol.

\tableref{tab:main} reports BCP, the three baselines, and PEM across the three configurations.
We highlight three findings.
First, \textbf{TS produces essentially no Dice change on any configuration} ($|\Delta\mathrm{Dice}|\!\le\!0.03$), consistent with the theoretical prediction that recalibration cannot alter a region-overlap metric whose argmax is invariant to monotone logit rescaling.
Any gain PEM achieves must therefore arise from parameter updates at decision-boundary voxels, not from confidence rescaling.
Second, \textbf{PEM attains the largest mean Dice gain on Pancreas-CT 20\% and LA 5\%} ($+1.12$ and $+1.43$, versus $+0.50$/$+0.21$ for PL-FT/SC on Pancreas and $+1.09$/$+0.71$ on LA 5\%), together with the largest HD95 reductions on the same two settings.
Third, on the most saturated configuration \textbf{LA 10\%} (BCP baseline $89.40\%$), \textbf{PL-FT marginally outperforms PEM} ($+0.80$ vs. $+0.64$ Dice), with overlapping bootstrap $95\%$ confidence intervals.
This ordering is consistent with the structural argument of Sec.~\ref{sec:method}: as the residual entropy budget shrinks, the soft gradient $\partial H/\partial p$ decreases with it while hard pseudo-label gradients remain $O(1)$, and the two methods become complementary in the saturation limit.
PEM attains its largest advantage where the residual entropy budget is largest --- the regime characterized by Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}.

A three-seed run of PEM under the same protocol gives $84.01\!\pm\!0.00$ (Pancreas-CT), $88.59\!\pm\!0.25$ (LA 5\%), and $90.02\!\pm\!0.07$ (LA 10\%) Dice; all three intervals exclude the BCP baseline.
On LA, PEM uses confident-mode masking ($\tau\!=\!0.95$) to concentrate updates on the $\sim$6\% boundary shell; on Pancreas-CT the full and confident modes are statistically indistinguishable because the $p(1{-}p)$ factor in Eq.~\ref{eq:dHdz} already gates the gradient to the boundary shell without an explicit mask.

\paragraph{Per-case statistical significance.}
Because the test set is small ($N\!=\!18$ Pancreas, $N\!=\!20$ LA), we additionally treat each test volume as a paired observation $(\mathrm{Dice}_{\text{BCP}}^{(i)},\, \mathrm{Dice}_{\text{BCP+PEM}}^{(i)})$ and report a paired analysis on $\Delta_i$.
On all three configurations, the per-case Wilcoxon signed-rank test rejects $H_0: \mathrm{median}(\Delta){=}0$ at $p\!<\!0.025$; the $95\%$ bias-corrected bootstrap confidence interval for $\bar\Delta$ has lower bound $\geq\!+0.18$ Dice; and PEM improves on $13$--$16$ of $18$--$20$ test cases (Tab.~\ref{tab:significance}).
The paired effect size is moderate to large ($d_z\!\in\![0.57,\,0.71]$), consistent with a distributed boundary refinement rather than a small number of high-leverage outliers.

\subsection{Ablations}
\vspace{-2pt}

\tableref{tab:ablation} isolates the three PEM hyperparameters on the LA 5\% checkpoint, the configuration on which each axis is most informative.
Each block varies one hyperparameter and fixes the others at their reported default.

\textbf{Masking threshold $\tau$ (rows a).}
Full-volume entropy minimization ($\tau\!=\!1$) improves Dice by only $+0.51$ on LA because the gradient contributions from the $\sim$$92\%$ saturated voxels --- while individually small --- collectively dilute the boundary-shell signal at this level of saturation.
Tightening the mask to $\tau\!=\!0.95$ increases the Dice gain to $+1.39$ (a $2.7\times$ improvement) and the HD95 reduction from $30\%$ to $44\%$, indicating that selective masking is the dominant factor when the base model is highly saturated.
Tighter thresholds ($\tau\!=\!0.999,\,0.99$) discard too much of the boundary shell, and a looser threshold ($\tau\!=\!0.9$) includes saturated-interior voxels whose gradient noise reduces the effective signal-to-noise ratio.
The optimal threshold aligns with the $6$--$8\%$ boundary-shell width reported in Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}.

\textbf{Learning rate (rows b).}
PEM exhibits a narrow learning-rate window.
Below $5\!\times\!10^{-6}$ the BatchNorm-affine update magnitude is small relative to the per-batch gradient noise, and the test Dice drops by $0.6$--$1.0\%$ within the first epoch, at which point the collapse-protection safeguard ($\Delta\!<\!{-}0.5\%$) halts fine-tuning.
Above $5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ (verified separately; see Sec.~\ref{sec:method}) the step size is large enough to relocate the decision boundary rather than refine it, and the model loses calibration within one epoch.
The two-order-of-magnitude window between these regimes is consistent with the interpretation of PEM as a bounded update to a converged optimum.

\textbf{Epoch budget (rows c).}
Improvement is monotonic up to $E\!=\!8$ (peak Dice $88.93$, $\Delta\!=\!{+}1.61$) and decreases thereafter as entropy collapse begins to consume confident voxels.
We report the value at $E\!=\!5$, which is conservative --- it leaves $0.21$ Dice on the table relative to the empirical peak --- but is fixed in advance from labeled-fold cross-validation and therefore does not rely on test-set selection.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
\vspace{-2pt}

We make two precise claims and verify both empirically.
\textbf{(C1)} The three converged BCP checkpoints we study satisfy $\rho_f(0.99)\!\in\![0.894,\,0.948]$, whereas a supervised-only baseline trained on the same labels attains $\rho_f(0.99)\!=\!0.552$ --- a $34$--$40$\,percentage-point gap observed consistently across two datasets and three label fractions, and the precondition under which PEM is well-conditioned (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}, Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}).
\textbf{(C2)} Whenever (C1) holds, a single post-hoc entropy-minimization fine-tune --- without labels, pseudo-labels, architectural changes, or test-set hyperparameter tuning --- improves Dice by $+0.6$ to $+1.4$ and HD95 by $27$--$43\%$, under a fixed compute budget of at most $5.1$\,min per configuration (Tab.~\ref{tab:main}).

\paragraph{Failure modes.}
The diagnostic $\rho_f(0.99)$ delineates the regime in which PEM is well-posed; outside this regime, PEM fails in two distinct ways, both predicted by the theoretical grounding of Sec.~\ref{sec:method}.
\textbf{(F1) Diffuse-entropy collapse.}
When $\rho_f(0.99)\!\lesssim\!0.6$, as on the supervised-only baseline ($\rho_f\!=\!0.552$, Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}), the residual entropy is scattered across interior regions rather than concentrated at decision boundaries.
The PEM gradient is then dominated by voxels where the model is genuinely confused, and sharpening reinforces existing errors; in our experiments the collapse-protection safeguard halts every such run within a single epoch, on the supervised baseline at every learning rate tested and on LA 5\% at $\mathrm{lr}\!\le\!10^{-6}$ (Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}, rows b$^\dagger$).
\textbf{(F2) Vanishing-entropy diminishing returns.}
As the base checkpoint becomes more saturated, $\rho_f(0.99)\!\to\!1$ but the total residual entropy $\sum_i H_i$ decreases; the soft PEM gradient, which scales with this budget via the $p(1\!-\!p)$ factor in Eq.~\ref{eq:dHdz}, correspondingly shrinks, while hard pseudo-label gradients remain $O(1)$.
This is the regime in which PL-FT becomes competitive with PEM, as observed on the most saturated configuration LA 10\% (Tabs.~\ref{tab:main} and~\ref{tab:significance}).
Both failure modes are predicted by the structural argument and can be ruled out in advance by a single label-free measurement of $\rho_f$ on the unlabeled training set.

\paragraph{Other limitations.}
Our empirical results are restricted to BCP-family checkpoints because they are the only ones publicly released on these benchmarks; whether (C1) holds for MOST-~\cite{liu2024most} or DyCON-style~\cite{assefa2025dycon} checkpoints is an open question that we intend to address as those checkpoints become available.
A second limitation is the dataset-specific learning rate, which we select via labeled-fold cross-validation; an adaptive step size derived directly from the entropy histogram of the base checkpoint is left to future work.
