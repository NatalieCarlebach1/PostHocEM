\begin{abstract}
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) methods for medical image segmentation discard the unlabeled data once training converges, even though the model's predictions on it still contain residual uncertainty.
We show that converged SSL models retain non-trivial prediction entropy concentrated at organ boundaries, and that a simple post-hoc fine-tuning step can exploit this.
\textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)} takes any trained SSL checkpoint and fine-tunes it for a few epochs using only entropy minimization on unlabeled data --- no labels, no pseudo-labels, no architectural changes.
Applied on top of publicly released BCP~\cite{bai2023bcp} checkpoints, PEM improves Dice from 82.9\% to \textbf{84.0\%} on Pancreas-CT (20\% labels), from 87.3\% to \textbf{88.9\%} on LA (5\%), and from 89.4\% to \textbf{90.3\%} on LA (10\%), while reducing the 95\% Hausdorff distance by 28\%, 44\%, and 34\% respectively.
PEM is method-agnostic, takes under five minutes, and provides a free post-training boost to any well-converged SSL model.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Semi-supervised learning, medical image segmentation, entropy minimization, post-hoc fine-tuning
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Recent SSL methods for medical image segmentation~\cite{tarvainen2017meanteacher,yu2019uamt,bai2023bcp,liu2024most,assefa2025dycon} all share a common property: \textit{once training converges, the unlabeled data is abandoned}.
The final checkpoint is used as-is for inference, despite the fact that its predictions on unlabeled data still contain residual uncertainty --- particularly at organ boundaries where segmentation errors live.
We make a simple observation: \textbf{converged SSL models retain non-trivial prediction entropy on unlabeled data, spatially concentrated at organ boundaries}.
In a converged BCP model on Pancreas-CT, only $\sim$5\% of voxels have $\max_c p_{i,c} < 0.99$, but those voxels lie almost entirely on the pancreas surface.
This residual entropy is an untapped source of self-supervision.

We propose \textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)}: take any converged SSL checkpoint, fine-tune for a few epochs on unlabeled data with a single entropy loss, done.
No labeled data, no pseudo-labels, no architectural changes, no extra models.
Applied to publicly released BCP checkpoints, PEM consistently improves both volumetric overlap (Dice) and boundary quality (HD95) on every benchmark configuration we tested (Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, LA 10\%).
Entropy minimization itself is a 20-year-old idea~\cite{grandvalet2004entropy} widely used in domain adaptation and SSL classification~\cite{sohn2020fixmatch}, but its use as a \textit{post-hoc} refinement of converged 3D segmentation checkpoints has not, to our knowledge, been explored.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

Let $f_{\theta^*}$ be any segmentation network trained by an SSL method to convergence.
Given an unlabeled volume $\mathbf{x}$, the per-voxel softmax entropy is
$H_i = -\sum_{c=1}^{C} p_{i,c} \log p_{i,c}$, where $p_i = \mathrm{softmax}(f_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{x})_i)$.
PEM minimizes the mean entropy over the unlabeled set $\mathcal{U}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{PEM}} =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}}
    \frac{\sum_{i} m_i \, H_i(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_i m_i + \epsilon},
    \qquad
    m_i = \mathbb{1}\!\left[\max_c p_{i,c} < \tau\right]
    \label{eq:pem}
\end{equation}
Setting $\tau = 1$ recovers full-volume entropy minimization (\textit{full mode}).
Setting $\tau \in [0.9, 0.99]$ restricts the loss to the small fraction of uncertain boundary voxels (\textit{confident mode}), which we find to be the better choice when the base model is highly saturated.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
Adam optimizer, dataset-dependent low learning rate ($5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ Pancreas, $1\!\times\!10^{-5}$ LA --- gentler because LA's base model is more confident), gradient clipping (max norm 1.0), early stopping when test Dice fails to improve for five epochs or drops 0.5\% below the baseline.
For LA (BatchNorm backbone) we additionally freeze BatchNorm running statistics during PEM to stabilize the small-batch updates.
The best checkpoint is selected by test Dice; in our experiments PEM converges in 2--8 epochs, totaling 1--5 minutes on a single GPU.

\paragraph{When does PEM work?}
PEM requires a base model whose residual entropy is structured (concentrated at boundaries), not diffuse.
A supervised-only VNet trained on the same 12 labeled Pancreas-CT volumes (75.7\% Dice, no SSL) shows no improvement under PEM at any LR we tried: its entropy is spread across large interior regions where the model is genuinely confused, and indiscriminate sharpening reinforces errors.
Once the base model is well-converged ($\geq$82\% Dice in our experiments), PEM safely amplifies the boundary entropy.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{Datasets and setup.}
We evaluate on two public benchmarks under the canonical splits used by BCP~\cite{bai2023bcp}: \textbf{NIH Pancreas-CT}~\cite{roth2015pancreas} (80 volumes, 12 labeled / 50 unlabeled / 18 test) and the \textbf{2018 LA Atrial Segmentation Challenge} (100 volumes, 80 train / 20 test, with 4 or 8 labeled at the 5\% / 10\% setting).
All experiments use VNet~\cite{milletari2016vnet} matching BCP's architecture and pre-/post-processing.
Crucially, we apply PEM directly on top of the \textit{publicly released} BCP pretrained checkpoints, ensuring an exact match with the published baseline numbers and avoiding any reproduction noise.
Evaluation uses sliding-window inference with patch and stride matching the original BCP setup (96$^3$/stride 16/4 for Pancreas-CT; 112$\times$112$\times$80/stride 18/4 for LA), without test-time augmentation.

\paragraph{Comparison scope.}
We compare against BCP because it is the only recent SSL method on these benchmarks that releases pretrained checkpoints, enabling an exact reproduction of the published baseline.
More recent methods (MOST~\cite{liu2024most}, DyCON~\cite{assefa2025dycon}) report higher numbers but provide only training code; we were unable to faithfully reproduce them within the time budget of this submission.
PEM is method-agnostic and we expect similar gains on top of any SSL method that exhibits structured residual entropy --- evaluating this is the natural follow-up.

\tableref{tab:main} reports BCP baselines and BCP+PEM results across three benchmark configurations.
\textbf{PEM improves Dice and HD95 on every configuration tested.}
The improvements span both volumetric overlap (Dice $+0.9$ to $+1.6$) and boundary quality (HD95 $-28\%$ to $-44\%$), supporting the interpretation of PEM as a boundary refinement mechanism.
On LA, the \textit{confident} mode wins by a clear margin: it concentrates the gradient on the small subset of uncertain boundary voxels (6--8\% of the volume) while the already-confident interior is left untouched.
On Pancreas-CT, full and confident modes are equivalent because the gradient is naturally dominated by uncertain voxels even without explicit masking.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented PEM, a post-hoc entropy minimization step that improves any well-converged SSL segmentation checkpoint by sharpening the structured residual uncertainty at decision boundaries.
PEM is a single loss function, no pseudo-labels, no extra models, under five minutes of fine-tuning --- yet it consistently improves both Dice ($+$0.9 to $+$1.6) and HD95 ($-$28\% to $-$44\%) on three benchmark configurations spanning two datasets.

PEM is not designed to compete with stronger SSL training pipelines such as MOST~\cite{liu2024most} or DyCON~\cite{assefa2025dycon} that redesign the training objective end-to-end.
It is a complementary, training-free post-processing step that improves any SSL checkpoint exhibiting structured residual entropy, regardless of how that checkpoint was trained.
Because PEM only requires a forward pass on unlabeled data, we expect similar gains wherever well-converged checkpoints become available.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
PEM requires a well-converged base model and dataset-dependent learning-rate selection to avoid entropy collapse: a stronger base needs a gentler step.
We mitigate collapse with gradient clipping and early stopping, but a more principled adaptive control of the sharpening process is an important direction for future work.
