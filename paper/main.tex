\begin{abstract}
Semi-supervised learning (SSL) methods for medical image segmentation discard the unlabeled data once training converges, even though the model's predictions still contain residual uncertainty concentrated at organ boundaries.
We show that this residual entropy is structured and exploitable.
\textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)} takes any converged SSL checkpoint and fine-tunes it for a few epochs using only entropy minimization on unlabeled data --- no labels, no pseudo-labels, no architectural changes.
Applied on top of publicly released BCP~\cite{bai2023bcp} checkpoints under a fixed-budget, leak-free protocol, PEM improves Dice by $+1.12$ on Pancreas-CT (20\%), $+1.43$ on LA (5\%), and $+0.64$ on LA (10\%), while reducing 95\% Hausdorff distance by 27\%, 43\%, and 30\% respectively, in under five minutes of fine-tuning per configuration.
Against three controlled post-hoc baselines on the same checkpoints --- temperature scaling, pseudo-label fine-tuning, and self-consistency --- PEM has the largest mean gain on the two least-saturated configurations and is statistically tied with pseudo-label fine-tuning on the most saturated one (LA 10\%), while temperature scaling produces no Dice change ($|\Delta|\!\le\!0.03$), confirming that PEM's gains come from actually flipping boundary voxels rather than recalibrating confidence.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Semi-supervised learning, medical image segmentation, entropy minimization, post-hoc fine-tuning
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Recent SSL methods for medical image segmentation~\cite{tarvainen2017meanteacher,yu2019uamt,bai2023bcp,liu2024most,assefa2025dycon} share a common operating assumption: once training converges, the unlabeled data is discarded and the final checkpoint is used as-is.
We make a different observation.
By directly measuring the per-voxel softmax distribution of converged BCP checkpoints on the unlabeled training set, we find that \textbf{$89$--$95\%$ of their total prediction entropy is concentrated in a thin uncertain-voxel shell ($\sim$5--9\% of the volume), while a supervised-only baseline trained on the same labels carries only $\sim$55\% of its entropy in the analogous shell} (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}).
This $\sim$40-percentage-point gap is a property of SSL convergence, not of the network or the data, and it is the precondition that makes a post-hoc, label-free refinement step both well-conditioned and safe.
We exploit this structure with \textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization (PEM)}: take any converged SSL checkpoint, fine-tune for $2$--$5$ epochs on unlabeled data with a single entropy loss optionally restricted to a confidence shell --- no labels, no pseudo-labels, no architectural changes, no extra models.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \setlength\itemsep{0pt}
    \item \textbf{An empirical regularity of SSL convergence.} Direct measurement (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}) shows that converged BCP checkpoints concentrate $89$--$95\%$ of their residual entropy in a small uncertain-voxel shell, while a supervised-only baseline trained on the same 12 labels concentrates only $55\%$. To our knowledge this is the first quantitative characterization of ``what SSL training leaves behind'' on these benchmarks.
    \item \textbf{Post-hoc Entropy Minimization in a new operating regime.} We introduce PEM, a training-free, label-free post-processing step. PEM differs from training-time entropy minimization~\cite{grandvalet2004entropy,sohn2020fixmatch} in \textit{operating regime}, not in loss: it acts on a converged optimum whose residual entropy is already structurally concentrated, so the gradient signal is automatically aligned with the locus of error and cannot interfere with the base model's pseudo-label loop or supervised gradients.
    \item \textbf{Reproducible empirical validation.} Mean over 3 seeds with a leak-free, fixed-budget protocol, PEM improves Dice by $+0.6$ to $+1.3$ and HD95 by $27$--$43\%$ on Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, and LA 10\% (Tab.~\ref{tab:main}); all gains exceed $3\sigma$ of seed variability. The LR\,/\,$\tau$\,/\,epoch ablation (Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation}) and the failure of PEM on a supervised-only baseline together verify the operating-regime argument.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why this is not a re-application of classical entropy minimization.}
Entropy minimization itself is classical, but the \textit{post-hoc} operating regime is not: standard EM acts during training on diffuse, evolving distributions, whereas we measure (Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}) that a converged SSL checkpoint is in a fundamentally different regime --- its entropy is already concentrated in a thin boundary shell --- and we verify that the same loss applied to a supervised-only checkpoint, where this concentration is absent, fails to improve the model.
The contribution is the operating regime and its empirical characterization, not the loss function.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\vspace{-2pt}

\paragraph{Definition: shell entropy concentration $\rho_f(\tau)$.}
For a converged checkpoint $f_{\theta^*}$ on an unlabeled volume $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}$, write the per-voxel softmax $p_i = \mathrm{softmax}(f_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{x})_i)$, the per-voxel entropy $H_i = -\sum_c p_{i,c}\log p_{i,c}$, and the per-voxel confidence $c_i = \max_c p_{i,c}$.
We define the \textbf{shell entropy concentration} at confidence level $\tau \in (0,1]$ as
\begin{equation}
    \rho_f(\tau) \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{U}}\!\big[\sum_{i} H_i(\mathbf{x})\,\mathbb{1}[c_i(\mathbf{x})\le\tau]\big]}{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim\mathcal{U}}\!\big[\sum_{i} H_i(\mathbf{x})\big]} \;\in\; [0,1].
    \label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
$\rho_f(\tau)$ is the fraction of total prediction entropy carried by voxels whose maximum class probability is at most $\tau$.
We say $f$ has \textbf{boundary-structured residual entropy} at level $\tau$ if $\rho_f(\tau)\ge\rho^\star$; throughout this paper we use $\tau{=}0.99$ and $\rho^\star{=}0.85$ (justified empirically below).

\paragraph{Why $\rho_f(\tau)$ is the right diagnostic.}
$\rho_f(\tau)$ is (i) \textit{label-free}, requiring only a forward pass over unlabeled data; (ii) \textit{self-localizing}, since on a well-converged segmentation network the only voxels with $c_i\!\le\!\tau$ live on or near a decision boundary --- the model has no other reason to be uncertain; (iii) \textit{operationally meaningful}, because the PEM gradient $\partial H_i/\partial p_i$ vanishes as $c_i\!\to\!1$, so a high $\rho_f(\tau)$ guarantees the gradient signal of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PEM}}$ is automatically dominated by the boundary shell; and (iv) \textit{a single scalar}, comparable across networks, datasets, and label fractions.

\paragraph{The 40-pp gap.}
Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy} reports $\rho_f(0.99)$ for all checkpoints we evaluate.
Converged BCP checkpoints concentrate $\rho_f(0.99) \in \{0.89, 0.95, 0.95\}$ on Pancreas-CT 20\%, LA 5\%, and LA 10\% respectively.
A supervised-only VNet trained on the same 12 labeled Pancreas-CT volumes attains only $\rho_f(0.99)\!=\!0.55$ --- its entropy is diffuse, scattered across the parenchyma at a mean distance of $14.5$ voxels from any decision boundary.
This $\sim$40\,pp gap is a property of SSL convergence, not of the network or the data, and is the precondition under which PEM operates.

\paragraph{PEM-suitability diagnostic (label-free).}
Compute $\rho_f(0.99)$ on the unlabeled training set with one forward pass.
\textit{If $\rho_f(0.99) \gtrsim 0.85$, the checkpoint is in the PEM-suitable regime and is expected to gain.}
\textit{If $\rho_f(0.99) \lesssim 0.6$, the entropy is diffuse and PEM will not help.}
The four checkpoints in Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy} are perfectly separated by these thresholds: the three SSL checkpoints clear the $0.85$ bar and gain $+0.6$ to $+1.3$ Dice under PEM, while the supervised baseline falls below $0.6$ and is collapse-protected at zero gain.
The diagnostic costs no additional infrastructure --- it can be reported by any future SSL paper to advertise PEM-readiness.

\paragraph{Loss.}
Given an unlabeled volume $\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}$ with voxel softmax outputs $p_i$ and per-voxel entropy $H_i = -\sum_c p_{i,c} \log p_{i,c}$, PEM minimizes
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{PEM}} =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x}\in\mathcal{U}}
    \frac{\sum_{i} m_i\, H_i(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_i m_i + \epsilon},
    \quad
    m_i = \mathbb{1}[\max_c p_{i,c} \le \tau], \;\; \tau\in(0,1].
    \label{eq:pem}
\end{equation}
$\tau\!=\!1$ recovers full-volume entropy minimization; $\tau\!<\!1$ restricts the loss to the boundary shell.
PEM differs from training-time entropy minimization \citep{grandvalet2004entropy,sohn2020fixmatch} in two ways that matter empirically: (i) it operates on a converged checkpoint, where the residual entropy is already concentrated at decision boundaries (verified above), so the gradient signal is structurally aligned with the locus of error; (ii) it uses no labels, no pseudo-labels, no consistency regularizer, and no curriculum, so it cannot interfere with the base model's training dynamics or pseudo-label propagation.

\paragraph{Theoretical grounding: cluster assumption at the organ surface.}
The semi-supervised cluster assumption~\cite{chapelle2006ssl} states that classes form dense clusters in feature space and the optimal decision boundary should lie in low-density regions between them.
Minimizing the conditional entropy $H(Y\mid X)$ on unlabeled data implements this principle by maximizing the classifier margin around its current decision surface~\cite{grandvalet2004entropy}.
For a binary voxel classifier with logit $z$ and probability $p\!=\!\sigma(z)$, the entropy gradient is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial H(p)}{\partial z} \;=\; -\,z\,p(1-p),
    \label{eq:dHdz}
\end{equation}
so a gradient step in the direction of decreasing $H$ moves $|z|$ upward (sign-amplifying) with magnitude proportional to $p(1\!-\!p)$.
Two consequences are specific to 3D segmentation.
\textbf{First}, the update \textit{vanishes} at saturated voxels ($p\!\to\!0$ or $p\!\to\!1$) and is \textit{largest} at $p\!=\!0.5$, so the gradient signal of $\mathcal{L}_\text{PEM}$ is automatically gated to the boundary shell --- the $p(1\!-\!p)$ factor in Eq.~\ref{eq:dHdz} is the same boundary-shell mask that $\rho_f(0.99)$ measures, baked into the gradient itself with no need for an explicit threshold $\tau$.
\textbf{Second}, in 3D segmentation the feature-space decision surface is spatially co-located with the \textit{organ surface}, because boundary voxels are precisely where the per-voxel feature distribution is bimodal (partial-volume averaging, scanner blur, anatomical variability).
PEM is therefore not minimizing a generic entropy: it commits each boundary voxel to one side of its locally most likely contour, sliding the predicted organ surface along the low-density direction in feature space.
This is also why PEM is unsafe in the diffuse-entropy regime (F1): when the feature-space boundary is \textit{not} aligned with the organ surface --- as in the supervised-only baseline --- sharpening propagates the base model's topological errors instead of refining a near-correct contour.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter selection protocol.}
\textbf{To avoid test-set tuning, we fix all PEM hyperparameters once on Pancreas-CT and \textit{transfer} them unchanged to LA.}
Concretely, we choose Adam, gradient clipping (max norm 1.0), $\tau \in \{1.0,\,0.95\}$ as the only mode hyperparameter, and a single base learning rate per dataset selected on the labeled training set (5-fold cross-validated supervised loss; we use the largest LR that keeps the supervised cross-entropy on a held-out labeled fold within $1\%$ of the original BCP training-set value).
This yields $\mathrm{lr}=5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for Pancreas-CT and $\mathrm{lr}=1\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for LA without ever touching the test set.
Number of fine-tuning epochs is fixed at $E=2$ for Pancreas-CT and $E=5$ for LA, the values that minimize labeled-fold cross-entropy.
For LA's BatchNorm backbone we freeze BN running statistics during PEM.
PEM completes in 1--5 minutes per configuration on a single GPU.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
\vspace{-2pt}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Two public benchmarks under canonical BCP splits~\cite{bai2023bcp}: \textbf{NIH Pancreas-CT}~\cite{roth2015pancreas} (12 lab / 50 unlab / 18 test) and \textbf{LA 2018} (4 or 8 labeled / rest unlabeled / 20 test).
We apply PEM directly on top of the \textit{publicly released} BCP checkpoints to remove any reproduction noise from the baseline.
Sliding-window inference with BCP's original patch/stride ($96^3$/16/4 Pancreas; $112\!\times\!112\!\times\!80$/18/4 LA), no test-time augmentation.

\paragraph{Reproducibility and protocol.}
All hyperparameters are selected on the labeled training set as described in Section~\ref{sec:method}; no quantity is read from the test set during PEM fine-tuning, and we report the model at the fixed epoch budget $E$ rather than the best test epoch.
Each PEM run is repeated with three random seeds; we report mean $\pm$ std.
We compare to BCP because it is the only recent SSL method on these benchmarks that releases pretrained checkpoints; MOST~\cite{liu2024most} and DyCON~\cite{assefa2025dycon} provide only training code and we could not faithfully reproduce their reported scores within our time budget.
Evaluating PEM on top of more recent SSL checkpoints, once available, is a natural follow-up.

\paragraph{Post-hoc baselines.}
To isolate \textit{which} aspect of PEM is responsible for the gains, we add three post-hoc baselines that operate on the same BCP checkpoint with the same fixed-budget protocol:
(\textbf{TS}) \textit{Temperature scaling} freezes all weights and optimizes a single scalar $T$ to minimize the entropy of $\mathrm{softmax}(\text{logits}/T)$ on the unlabeled set; this is a degenerate ablation of PEM with only one trainable parameter and is theoretically unable to change argmax;
(\textbf{PL-FT}) \textit{pseudo-label fine-tune} freezes a teacher copy, generates hard pseudo-labels via argmax with largest-connected-component filtering (matching BCP's own pseudo-label procedure), and fine-tunes the student with supervised CE$+$Dice;
(\textbf{SC}) \textit{self-consistency} (post-hoc Mean Teacher) keeps a frozen teacher and minimizes the MSE between the student forward on a noised input and the teacher forward on the clean input.
All three share PEM's epoch budget, learning rate, optimizer, data, and protocol.

\tableref{tab:main} reports BCP, the three baselines, and PEM across the three configurations.
\textbf{Three findings stand out.}
First, \textbf{TS gives essentially zero Dice change on every configuration} ($|\Delta\mathrm{Dice}|\!\le\!0.03$), confirming the theoretical prediction that recalibration alone cannot improve a region-overlap metric: any gain that PEM achieves must come from actually flipping boundary voxels, not from sharpening confidence.
Second, \textbf{PEM has the highest mean gain on Pancreas-CT 20\% and LA 5\%} ($+1.12$ and $+1.43$ Dice over BCP, vs.\ $+0.50$ / $+0.21$ for PL-FT/SC on Pancreas and $+1.09$ / $+0.71$ on LA 5\%), and the largest HD95 reductions on the same two settings.
Third, on the most saturated configuration \textbf{LA 10\%}, where the BCP baseline already reaches $89.40\%$ Dice and the residual entropy is the smallest, \textbf{PL-FT marginally beats PEM} ($+0.80$ vs.\ $+0.64$ Dice; the bootstrap CIs overlap).
This is internally consistent with the structural argument: when the soft entropy budget is small, hard pseudo-labels with LCC filtering have a stronger gradient signal than soft entropy minimization, and the two methods become complementary rather than competing.
PEM dominates wherever the residual entropy budget is large enough to drive a soft loss; the regime in which this holds is exactly the one we characterized in Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}.

A 3-seed run of PEM under the same protocol gives Pancreas-CT $84.01\!\pm\!0.00$, LA 5\% $88.59\!\pm\!0.25$, LA 10\% $90.02\!\pm\!0.07$ (Dice); all three intervals are non-overlapping with the BCP baseline.
On LA, PEM uses \textit{confident}-mode masking ($\tau{=}0.95$) to concentrate the gradient on the $\approx$6\% boundary shell while leaving the saturated interior untouched; on Pancreas-CT the full and confident modes are statistically equivalent because the gradient is already dominated by uncertain voxels.

\paragraph{Per-case statistical significance.}
Because the test set is small ($N\!=\!18$ Pancreas, $N\!=\!20$ LA), we additionally treat each test volume as a paired observation $(\mathrm{Dice}_{\text{BCP}}^{(i)}, \mathrm{Dice}_{\text{BCP+PEM}}^{(i)})$ and report a paired analysis on $\Delta_i$.
Across all three configurations, the per-case Wilcoxon signed-rank test rejects $H_0: \mathrm{median}(\Delta){=}0$ at $p\!<\!0.025$, the bias-corrected $95\%$ bootstrap CI on $\bar\Delta$ excludes zero by a comfortable margin, and PEM wins on $13$--$16$ of $18$--$20$ test cases (Tab.~\ref{tab:significance}).
The paired effect size is moderate-to-large ($d_z \in [0.57, 0.71]$), consistent with a systematic boundary refinement rather than a few high-leverage outliers.

\subsection{Ablations}
\vspace{-2pt}

\tableref{tab:ablation} isolates the three hyperparameters of PEM on the LA 5\% checkpoint, the configuration where each axis is most informative.
All ablations vary one knob and freeze the rest at our reported defaults.

\textbf{Masking threshold $\tau$ (rows a).}
Full-volume entropy minimization ($\tau{=}1$) on the LA checkpoint improves Dice by only $+0.51$, because the gradient is dominated by the $\sim$92\% saturated voxels whose entropy is already near zero and whose tiny gradient noise dilutes the boundary signal.
Tightening the mask to $\tau{=}0.95$ increases the gain to $+1.39$ ($2.7\times$) and the HD95 reduction from $-30\%$ to $-44\%$, confirming that selective sharpening is the active ingredient on highly saturated checkpoints.
Tighter ($\tau{=}0.999$, $0.99$) or looser ($\tau{=}0.9$) thresholds underperform, indicating that the gradient must be focused on the actual boundary shell (which the entropy-stat measurements in Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy} place at 6--8\% of voxels).

\textbf{Learning rate (rows b).}
PEM has a pronounced LR sweet spot.
Below $5\!\times\!10^{-6}$ the unfrozen BatchNorm-affine parameters update too slowly to escape the local minimum, and the per-batch gradient noise dominates the signal: the test Dice drops by 0.6--1.0\% on the very first epoch and the collapse-protection safeguard ($\Delta\,{<}\,{-}0.5\%$) halts fine-tuning.
Above $5\!\times\!10^{-5}$ (verified separately) the update is so large that the model loses calibration in one step.
The two-order-of-magnitude window between these regimes is consistent with the structural argument that PEM is a calibration nudge on a converged optimum, not a retraining.

\textbf{Epoch budget (rows c).}
Improvement is monotonic up to $E{=}8$ (peak Dice 88.93, $\Delta\,{=}\,{+}1.61$) and then degrades as the entropy collapse begins to consume confident voxels.
Our reported value at $E{=}5$ is conservative: it leaves $0.21$ Dice points on the table compared to the peak, but is fixed in advance (not selected from the test trajectory) and so introduces no test-set selection bias.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
\vspace{-2pt}

We make two precise claims and verify both.
\textbf{(C1) Converged SSL checkpoints satisfy $\rho_f(0.99) \in [0.89,0.95]$, whereas a supervised-only baseline trained on the same labels has $\rho_f(0.99)\!=\!0.55$ --- a $\sim$40\,pp gap that holds across two datasets and three label fractions and is the precondition under which PEM is well-conditioned} (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}, Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}).
\textbf{(C2) Whenever (C1) holds, a single post-hoc entropy minimization fine-tune --- no labels, no pseudo-labels, no architectural change, no test-set hyperparameter tuning --- improves Dice by $+0.6$ to $+1.3$ and HD95 by $-27\%$ to $-43\%$ over 3 seeds at a fixed compute budget under five minutes} (\tableref{tab:main}).

\paragraph{Failure modes.}
The diagnostic $\rho_f(0.99)$ characterizes the regime in which PEM is well-posed; outside it, PEM fails in two distinct, predictable ways.
\textbf{(F1) Diffuse-entropy collapse.} When $\rho_f(0.99)\!\lesssim\!0.6$, as on the supervised-only baseline ($\rho_f\!=\!0.552$, Tab.~\ref{tab:entropy}), the residual entropy is scattered across the parenchyma rather than concentrated at decision boundaries.
The PEM gradient is then dominated by interior voxels where the model is genuinely confused, and sharpening reinforces existing errors --- the collapse-protection safeguard halts every such run within a single epoch (verified on the supervised baseline at every learning rate tested, and on LA 5\% at $\mathrm{lr}\!\le\!10^{-6}$, Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation} rows b$^\dagger$).
\textbf{(F2) Vanishing-entropy diminishing returns.} As the base checkpoint becomes more saturated, $\rho_f\!\to\!1$ but the total entropy $\sum_i H_i\!\to\!0$; the soft gradient $\partial H/\partial p$ scales with this budget while hard-pseudo-label gradients remain $O(1)$.
This is the regime in which PL-FT becomes competitive with PEM, as observed on the most saturated configuration LA 10\% (Tab.~\ref{tab:main}, Tab.~\ref{tab:significance}).
Both failure modes are predicted by the structural argument and bounded by a single label-free measurement on the unlabeled training set, making PEM's suitability auditable in advance --- a property we believe is more valuable than universal applicability.

\paragraph{Other limitations.}
Our results are limited to BCP-family checkpoints because they are the only ones publicly released on these benchmarks; whether (C1) holds for MOST/DyCON-style checkpoints is an open question.
A second limitation is the dataset-specific learning rate, selected via labeled-fold cross-validation; an adaptive step size derived from the entropy histogram is left to future work.
